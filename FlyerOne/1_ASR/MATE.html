Multi-Attribute Tradespace Exploration (MATE)

1.0 Overview

Description of the project and its objectives.

This document serves to illuminate the Multi-Attribute Tradespace Exploration (MATE) process implemented for the digital design and certification of a Group III Unmanned Logistics Drone for the United States Air Force (USAF). As a pioneering initiative in the realm of digital engineering, this project recognizes the paramount importance of a multi-attribute tradespace study to guide decision-making under various constraints and uncertainties.

The ambitious project entails leveraging cutting-edge digital engineering tools and DODAF models to create one of the world's most intricate digital threads. The core aim is to impart groundbreaking changes in aircraft design and certification approaches, significantly reducing time and human effort typically required for such endeavours.

The project involves an extensive team comprising software engineers, systems engineers, aerospace engineers, artificial intelligence engineer, program managers, quality assurance engineer, multiple product team leads, and subcontractor. With a timeline of 24 months, the team is geared towards innovating and overcoming challenges inherent in this landmark project, ultimately delivering digital-first engineering reviews.

This MATE process is an integral part of the decision-making framework that weighs the often competing attributes to deliver optimal design solutions. It encompasses the identification of design variables, development of evaluation metrics, application of robust MATE models and analysis techniques, and finally, the presentation of comprehensive results to aid in informed decision making.

Through this MATE Analysis, the project team aims to effectively navigate the complexities of this novel task, driving the USAF's unmanned logistics capabilities to new heights and setting the stage for future developments in the field of digital engineering.

2.0 Purpose of the MATE Analysis

A brief description of why the MATE analysis is needed, and what critical decisions will be based on its results.

The purpose of executing a Multi-Attribute Tradespace Exploration (MATE) for this project is to enable systematic evaluation of various design alternatives in the context of their performance, cost, and risk factors. By exploring these different parameters, the MATE analysis allows the project team to identify promising design directions for the Unmanned Logistics Drone. This holistic approach towards decision-making enables us to align with the USAF's requirements while ensuring efficiency and effectiveness in our innovative digital engineering process.

3.0 MATE Objectives

Outlines the goals for this exploration. This section provides the basis for creating metrics and selecting alternative strategies.

The objectives for this MATE analysis are three-fold:

1. Define and quantify the multi-dimensional tradespace: The analysis aims to delineate all feasible design solutions, considering various constraints and technical parameters.

2. Evaluate design alternatives: The MATE analysis will involve rigorous evaluation of these alternative solutions based on quantifiable metrics.

3. Inform decision-making: The ultimate goal of the MATE analysis is to provide data-backed insights to stakeholders, aiding them in making informed decisions for the digital design and certification process of the drone.

4.0 Definition of the Tradespace

Explanation of the bounds and boundaries of the tradespace. This includes all potential variables and resources included in the tradespace.

In this project, the tradespace constitutes the multi-dimensional design space where each dimension represents a design variable or a parameter that influences the performance and viability of the Unmanned Logistics Drone. The variables include, but are not limited to, the drone's payload capacity, flight range, energy consumption, material selection, hardware and software designs, and production costs. Constraints are primarily defined by technical feasibility, budgetary limitations and compliance with USAF requirements.

The fusion of these design variables and parameters constructs a complex tradespace, where each point represents a potential design solution. The exploration of this tradespace through the MATE process aids in identifying optimal design solutions that strike a balance between competing objectives and constraints.

Details on models used (e.g. DODAF, parametric models, simulation tools etc.) in the study including their objectives, assumptions, validity and expected output.

In the preliminary stages of this study, our focus lies on the utilization of simple parametric models. These are primarily developed in MATLAB to establish foundational design relationships and variables. These models incorporate key factors such as spectral and spatial data, physical parameters, and mission requirements, aiming to provide a basic understanding of the design variables' impacts on performance attributes.

As the project advances, we will progressively incorporate more complex data and tools into our analyses. This section specifies all design variables that will be considered in the trade-off analysis and any associated constraints or boundary conditions.

6.1 Design Variables

Design variables for the Unmanned Logistics Drone include technical parameters like payload capacity, flight range, energy efficiency, materials used, software reliability, hardware durability, and production costs. Each variable will be manipulated and tested within agreed upon limits during the design process.

Design variables are parameters that can be manipulated during the design process to influence the performance and cost-effectiveness of the drone. For the FlyerØne drone, these include:

  1. Crew: Always 1 (remote control from ground-based console).
  2. Length: Ranges from 7 to 8 ft (2.1 to 2.4 m). This small variance is to account for potential payload accommodation.
  3. Wingspan: Ranges from 26.5 to 28.5 ft (8.1 to 8.7 m). A larger wingspan can provide more lift but may increase drag.
  4. Engine Type: Always Jetcat P400 turbojets or equivalent, considering thrust and fuel efficiency.
  5. Number of Engines: Always 2 engines.
  6. Thrust per Engine: Ranges from 80 to 95 lbf (0.36 to 0.42 kN) to allow for payload and performance variability.
  7. Payload Doors: Binary variable - either present (1) or not present (0).
  8. Sensor Packages: Variable depending on the mission requirements, could range from minimal (1) to extensive (5).
  9. Landing Gear Design: Binary variable - either retractable (1) or fixed (0).
  10. Fuel Payload: Ranges from 100 to 200 lbs (45 to 91 kg), depending on the drone's structure and engine efficiency.
  11. Type of Ground Based Control: Depending on the operation needs, this can vary - simple remote control (1), advanced joystick-based system (2), or a comprehensive control suite with multiple screens (3).
  12. Type of Comms Link: Can range from basic radio frequency (RF) link (1) to advanced satellite communication link (2), considering range and reliability.
  13. Type of Fail-safe: Binary variable - either parachute system (1) or automated emergency landing system (0).

6.2 Constraints

Constraints are defined by factors such as technical feasibility, regulatory standards, budgetary limitations, the 24-month timeline, and the USAF's operational criteria. Detailed risk assessments are performed to ensure constraints are comprehensively understood and addressed.

One of the main constraints we will focus on for this effort is to ensure that we are keeping the Airworthiness Risk Hazard Score to “Serious” or below so as to only require a PEO signature for accepting risk and NOT to require a SAF/AQ signature for accepting risk.

Constraints are limitations that must be adhered to during the design process. For the FlyerØne drone, these include:

  1. Technical Feasibility: The drone's design and engineering must be achievable using current technology, knowledge, and practices. This includes aspects like materials, electronics, aerodynamics, and manufacturing techniques.
  2. Regulatory Standards: The drone must align with all relevant DoD 5160.61 airworthiness standards for UAS, as per the Department of Defense (DoD) guidelines.
  3. Budgetary Limitations: The total cost of the drone, including research, design, production, testing, and maintenance, must not exceed $1M USD. This constraint must be considered at every stage of the project to ensure the final product remains within the budget.
  4. Time Constraints: The drone must be fully designed, built, tested, and ready for operation within a 24-month timeline. This includes any necessary time for approvals and certifications.
  5. USAF's Operational Criteria: The drone must meet the operational needs and requirements of the United States Air Force (USAF). This can include aspects like reliability, durability, ease of maintenance, and mission compatibility.
  6. Airworthiness Risk Hazard Score: The design and operation of the drone must ensure that the Airworthiness Risk Hazard Score does not exceed a "Serious" level, as per the DoD 516C airworthiness process as shown below in Figure 1. This is to keep the risk acceptance level within the authority of the Program Executive Officer (PEO) and not require a signature from the Secretary of the Air Force for Acquisition (SAF/AQ).

These variables and constraints form the multi-dimensional tradespace that will undergo exploration, to identify viable solutions for the drone design.

7.0 Evaluation Metrics

A list of metrics that will be used to measure the effectiveness and efficiency of each design or strategy in the tradespace.

In the Multi-Attribute Tradespace Analysis for the FlyerØne drone project, we will employ a variety of evaluation metrics that are associated with the design and performance variables that have been outlined. These evaluation metrics are intended to comprehensively assess the viability, efficiency, and effectiveness of various design configurations.

The first set of metrics, referred to as Key Performance Parameters (KPPs), will be the primary determinants of the operational success of the drone design. These include range, payload, and speed. The range will be evaluated on the ability to meet a threshold of 300 miles with an objective of 400 miles. This metric is crucial in evaluating the drone's ability to fulfil long-distance logistics missions. Payload capacity, another pivotal metric, will be assessed on meeting a minimum threshold of 100 lbs and an ideal objective of 200 lbs, reflecting the drone's capacity to transport significant cargo. Lastly, the drone's speed is an important metric that influences mission timelines. The speed will be gauged against a threshold value of 50 mph with an objective of reaching 138.1 mph.

The second set of metrics, the Key System Attributes (KSAs), will provide insights into the structural and design elements of the drone. These comprise the maximum weight and wingspan of the drone. The maximum weight has a threshold of 500 lbs and an objective of 1000 lbs, balancing the drone's load-carrying capacity with its energy efficiency and maneuverability. The maximum wingspan, with a threshold of 26.5 ft and an objective of 28.5 ft, influences the lift generation and stability of the drone. These metrics will aid in assessing the drone's aerodynamic performance and energy requirements.

Finally, we will assess Additional Performance Attributes (APAs), which focus on the sensor specifications of the drone. These encompass sensor range and sensor resolution. Sensor range, with a threshold of 2 miles and an objective of 20 miles, is vital to the drone's situational awareness and its ability to execute long-range missions. Sensor resolution, with a threshold of 720p and an objective of 1080p, affects the quality of the image feed, a critical factor in operator situational awareness and decision-making.

In summary, the outlined evaluation metrics will form a robust basis for assessing the varied design configurations of the FlyerØne drone. A thorough assessment using these metrics will guide the project team in navigating the multi-dimensional tradespace and aid in identifying the most promising and efficient design solutions for this Group III unmanned logistics drone.

9.0 Conclusion

Summarization of final recommendations based on the MATE analysis. 

The MATE analysis conducted has provided significant insights into the trade-offs and potential optimization of the FlyerØne's design. The Pareto cost-capability trade-off charts in particular, have offered a detailed understanding of the balance that must be achieved between the cost of the drone and its key performance parameters (KPPs): range, payload, and speed.

From our analysis, we can make some preliminary conclusions regarding optimal design solutions. For example, it appears that an optimal trade-space exists where increasing payload does not substantially impact range capabilities. This could be an area of focus when further refining our design, as maintaining a high range while accommodating larger payloads would meet a significant portion of our operational requirements without drastically increasing costs.

In terms of speed, a moderate increase in cost seems to allow for substantial improvements. Therefore, investing more in engine performance might be a worthy consideration, especially for missions that require rapid deployment or fast turnaround times.

Finally, in relation to the design variables, it seems that our choice of communication link and the design of our landing gear significantly affect overall costs. By prioritizing a basic RF link over a more advanced satellite communication link where operationally feasible, or by opting for fixed landing gear over retractable designs in less demanding mission scenarios, we can maintain performance while managing costs.

In conclusion, the MATE analysis has enabled us to identify critical areas of focus and potential trade-offs for our design process. By leveraging this data-driven approach, we can further refine our design, making informed decisions that balance performance, cost, and operational requirements to create a versatile and effective Group III logistics drone. Further analysis and testing will continue as we refine our design choices and navigate towards our objective: creating an optimal unmanned logistics drone under the $1M USD cost cap.

Appendix A. Design Variables Detailed Explanation

This appendix will delve deeper into the nature and considerations of each design variable.

Crew: The drone is designed for remote operation from a ground-based console. This design variable is fixed at 1, signifying that the drone is fully automated and does not require an onboard crew. This decision is rooted in the modern trend towards automation in order to reduce risk to human life during operation. Moreover, unmanned vehicles provide a tactical advantage in terms of increased operational hours and enhanced resilience to challenging and adverse operational environments. It also reduces the logistical and economic burden of crew training and management, thus making the overall operation more cost-effective.

Length: The length of the drone, which ranges from 7 to 8 ft (2.1 to 2.4 m), plays a significant role in determining the payload accommodation and the overall aerodynamics of the vehicle. A longer drone could provide more internal space for payloads and onboard systems. However, a larger length could potentially affect the maneuverability of the drone, especially in complex environments. Therefore, the chosen range attempts to strike a balance between these considerations to ensure the drone can accommodate different payload sizes without significantly compromising its aerodynamic efficiency and maneuverability.

Wingspan: The drone's wingspan ranges from 26.5 to 28.5 ft (8.1 to 8.7 m). The wingspan directly impacts the lift generation, aerodynamic drag, and stability of the drone. A larger wingspan increases the lift generated, which can be beneficial for carrying larger payloads. However, it can also result in increased drag, which would decrease the drone's speed and increase fuel consumption. The specified range is thus designed to find the sweet spot between lift generation and drag to optimize flight performance and efficiency.

Engine Type: The use of Jetcat P400 turbojets, or equivalent engines, is based on their ability to provide the thrust needed for the drone's operation, coupled with the fuel efficiency required for long-range missions. Jet engines are known for their high power-to-weight ratio and reliability, which are crucial in unmanned operations. Choosing an engine type is a critical decision that impacts the drone's performance, mission capabilities, and overall cost, hence the selection of these specific engine types.

Number of Engines: The drone design always includes two engines. This design choice enhances redundancy, which improves reliability and operational performance, and allows for continued operation even if one engine fails.

Thrust per Engine: The thrust per engine ranges from 80 to 95 lbf (0.36 to 0.42 kN). This range ensures that the drone has sufficient power to carry various payloads and perform under different flight conditions. A wider range allows for more flexibility in adjusting to payload and performance requirements.

Payload Doors: This binary variable represents whether the drone has payload doors (1) or not (0). The presence of payload doors allows the drone to deliver payloads mid-flight, increasing its versatility and usability in different mission profiles.

Sensor Packages: The sensor packages range from minimal (1) to extensive (5) based on the mission requirements. The chosen range allows for mission customization, enabling the drone to carry different sensor packages for surveillance, communication, or navigation, depending on the operation.

Landing Gear Design: This binary variable indicates whether the drone has retractable landing gear (1) or fixed landing gear (0). Retractable landing gear can reduce drag during flight, which improves performance and fuel efficiency. Fixed landing gear can simplify the design and reduce costs but may impact performance.

Fuel Payload: The fuel payload ranges from 100 to 200 lbs (45 to 91 kg). This range accommodates different mission durations and ranges, depending on the drone's structure and engine efficiency. A larger fuel payload allows for longer missions but increases the overall weight of the drone.

Type of Ground Based Control: The type of ground control can vary - simple remote control (1), advanced joystick-based system (2), or a comprehensive control suite with multiple screens (3). This range caters to different levels of operational complexity and control precision, providing more control flexibility depending on the mission needs.
